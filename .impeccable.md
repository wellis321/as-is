# .impeccable.md

## Design Context

### Users
Mixed internal and external. Primary users are analysts, process improvement leads, and business change staff within a public sector organisation (likely a council or housing authority) — people who run workshops and interviews to document how processes currently work. Secondary audience: external partners, auditors, and potentially staff from other organisations if this becomes a product. Users range from technically confident to non-technical. They use the tool at a desk during or after workshops — not on mobile, not in a hurry, but they need clarity.

### Brand Personality
**Three words: Clear. Considered. Credible.**

Not governmental-dull, but government-trusted. The tool handles complex, important information (how organisations actually work) and needs to feel like it can be relied on. No whimsy. No decoration for decoration's sake. The interface should feel like a senior analyst designed it — someone who cares deeply about clarity and has good taste, not someone trying to be flashy.

### Aesthetic Direction
**GOV.UK-influenced but product-grade.** 

Reference: GOV.UK Design System — typographically restrained, high-contrast, accessible, deliberately unflashy. The best bits: clear heading hierarchy, legible body text, purposeful use of colour, excellent form design.

Differentiation: GOV.UK is a content-delivery system. This is a *product* — it needs to feel polished, confident, and like something another organisation would pay for or trust. So: better typography pairing, slightly warmer palette, more considered spacing rhythm, and a stronger visual identity through restraint rather than ornament.

**Typography**: IBM Plex Sans (body/UI) + IBM Plex Serif (display/headings on key surfaces). IBM Plex is used heavily in government/enterprise contexts, has genuine character, and reads beautifully at all sizes. Avoid: Inter, Roboto, system-ui defaults.

**Colour**: Deep slate-navy as dominant neutral (`oklch(22% 0.04 245)`), warm near-white background (`oklch(98% 0.005 245)`), a single teal accent (`oklch(55% 0.12 200)`) that references mapping/flow without being blue-corporate. Touches of amber for decision/warning states. No gradients. No dark mode (not needed for this audience and context).

**Layout**: Left-aligned. Comfortable max-width (800–1100px depending on context). Generous but asymmetric spacing — tighter groupings within sections, more generous separation between sections. No card-within-card nesting. Tables for structured data (this is a data tool). Forms that feel like they were carefully considered, not generated.

**Motion**: Near zero. A subtle opacity transition on interactive states. Nothing that bounces, slides, or draws attention away from the content.

**Anti-references**: 
- Not a startup SaaS with gradient CTAs and animated feature sections
- Not a dark-mode dashboard with neon accents  
- Not a generic admin panel with blue sidebar and icon-heavy navigation
- Not a consumer app trying to be friendly and approachable

### Design Principles

1. **Content is the interface** — The process maps, step titles, and connection data are what matters. The chrome should recede. Typography and whitespace do the heavy lifting; decoration doesn't.

2. **Credible at first glance** — An external auditor or a director from another council opening this for the first time should immediately think "this organisation takes this seriously." That comes from typographic precision, not visual flair.

3. **Accessible by default** — WCAG AA minimum. High-contrast text, visible focus states, no colour-only communication. Both technical and non-technical users, mixed familiarity with process tools.

4. **Purposeful restraint** — Every element earns its place. If a visual element doesn't help the user understand or act, it isn't there. This includes decorative borders, background patterns, and icon overuse.

5. **Product-grade polish** — The micro-details (spacing consistency, label alignment, empty states, error messages) signal whether this is a personal script or something others would trust. Invest in the details that users feel without noticing.
